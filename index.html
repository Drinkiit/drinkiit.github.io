Posted on 2017-05-03 | In Data Mining |

Introducing Lyman-Break Galaxies

Lyman-Break galaxies are star-forming galaxies that curiously disappear when viewed through certain filters. This phenomenon occurs as the combined result of two separate effects.

First, these galaxies contain abundant hydrogen gas. Excited by stellar radiation, this gas emits light within the Lyman series—but none at wavelengths shorter than the Lyman limit of 912 angstroms. Second, hot, newly formed stars emit light at wavelengths that are shorter than 912 angstroms, but this light is almost completely absorbed by neutral gases that typically surround star-forming regions. Viewed through a spectroscope, these Lyman-break galaxies are bright at wavelengths above (longer than) the Lyman limit, but vanishingly dim below it. Hence, the spectra of these galaxies “drop out,” or “break” at the Lyman limit. The location of this break in a galaxy’s spectrum depends on how distant the galaxy is, that is, how far the 912 angstrom line is “redshifted” into longer, optical wavelengths. By simply using a range of filters to view them through a telescope (rather than a spectroscope), Lyman-break galaxies will disappear when the Lyman limit in their light is reached. [Ref 1]

Isotonic Regression

Posted on 2017-03-18 | In Statistics |

The problem of isotonic regression on an ordered set is as follows. Given real numbers $\{ y_1, y_2, \dots, y_n \}$, the problem is to find $\{ m_1, m_2, \dots, m_n \}$ to minimize $\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i-m_i)^2$ subject to the restriction $m_1 \le m_2 \le \dots \le m_n$. A unique solution to this problem exists and can be obtained from the “pool adjacent violators” algorithm.

The Lighthouse Problem | and why sample mean is not a good estimator

Posted on 2017-02-13 | In Statistics |

This example comes from ‘Data analysis: a Bayesian tutorial’[1 P29]. It was originally studied by Gull (1988) who found this problem on a problems sheet for first-year undergraduates at Cambridge:

A lighthouse is somewhere off a piece of straight coastline at a position $\alpha$ along the shore and a distance $\beta$ out at sea. It emits a series of short highly collimated flashes at random intervals and hence at random azimuths. These pulses are intercepted on the coast by photo-detectors that record only the fact that a flash has occurred, but not the angle from which it came. $N$ flashes have so far been recorded at positions $x_k$. Where is the lighthouse?

The geometry of this problem is illustrated above. Give the nature of the lighthouse emissions, it’s reasonable to assign a uniform pdf for the azimuth of the $k$th datum $\theta_k$:

$$p(\theta_k | \alpha, \beta) = \frac{1}{\pi}$$
